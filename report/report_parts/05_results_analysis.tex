\section{Results and Analysis}\label{sec:results}

This section reports the main quantitative findings from the pipeline-only studies and from the direct comparison with the non-agentic baseline.

The two panels in Figure~\ref{fig:iter_peptides_combined} show the same structural trade-off: larger search budgets improve quality, but runtime grows much faster than score.

When the number of iterations increases from $5$ to $75$, the Top-1 score moves only from $0.88$ to $0.89$, while runtime rises from $0.92\,\mathrm{s}$ to $13.39\,\mathrm{s}$ (about $14.60\times$). This very slow quality growth strongly suggests that the current orchestrator policy is not extracting enough additional value from later rounds. In other words, extra rounds are expensive, but they are not yet sufficiently informative.

The same interpretation appears when increasing the number of generated peptides per round from $64$ to $512$: Top-1 score moves from $0.88$ to $0.89$, while runtime rises from $1.83\,\mathrm{s}$ to $14.70\,\mathrm{s}$ (about $8.00\times$). The search is stable and does improve quality, but the gain per unit of compute remains small at high budgets.

We know from the chemist information that peptides with small length are harder to find and very rare. Figure~\ref{fig:target_length} confirms that target length controls difficulty. Scores are lower at extreme short lengths and then rise toward a plateau for longer peptides: around $0.82$ at lengths $2$--$3$, around $0.93$ near length $11$. Runtime grows more smoothly, from about $1.90\,\mathrm{s}$ (length $2$) to $3.77\,\mathrm{s}$ (length $12$).

This result should be interpreted carefully: obtaining around $0.82$ even for length-$2$ targets can be seen as robustness, but it can also indicate that the biological ranking signal is not sufficiently discriminative in pathological short-length regimes. Said differently, part of this score may reflect scorer tolerance rather than truly meaningful biological plausibility, which is consistent with the proxy nature of the current evaluator.

\subsection{Pipeline vs Baseline by Target Length}\label{subsec:pipeline_vs_baseline}

The comparison evaluates four methods for target lengths $2$ to $11$: non-agentic baseline, pipeline with $1$ iteration, pipeline with $3$ iterations and pipeline with $10$ iterations.

}
\caption{Average combined score by target length (baseline vs iterative pipeline variants).}
\label{fig:baseline_score}
\end{figure}

The score trend in Figure~\ref{fig:baseline_score} is clearly regime-dependent.

For hard short targets, iteration gives a clear advantage. At length $2$, the baseline is at $0.58$, while the ten-iteration pipeline reaches $0.62$. At length $3$, the baseline is $0.60$ and the ten-iteration pipeline is $0.66$. The same pattern persists through mid lengths; for example at length $6$, the baseline is $0.61$ and the ten-iteration pipeline is $0.72$.

For easier long targets, the picture changes. At length $11$, the baseline reaches $0.83$, above the ten-iteration pipeline at $0.80$. This means iterative critique is not universally superior; it is most useful in difficult regimes, and can be unnecessary or slightly detrimental when direct generation is already strong.

\subsection{Interpretation}\label{subsec:expert_feedback}

Overall, the most robust conclusion is that iterative multi-expert search is most valuable in difficult target regimes, where it improves the score relatively to the non-agentic generation. The same experiments also expose clear improvement directions: a more sample-efficient orchestration strategy (to reduce slow score growth with additional rounds) and a more discriminative biological scorer for extreme short-length cases.
