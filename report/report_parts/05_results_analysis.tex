\section{Results and Analysis}\label{sec:results}

This section focuses on the key curves used to understand pipeline behavior (iterations, number of generated peptides, and target peptide length), then adds a direct comparison against the non-agentic baseline.
These are not the only analyses performed, but they are the most informative under the page constraints of this report.

Increasing iterations improves quality, but the gain remains gradual: Top-1 score moves from about $0.88$ to $0.89$. Runtime, however, grows sharply (from about $0.92$s to $13.39$s). This reflects a clear quality--time trade-off, with diminishing score gains at high iteration counts.

When more candidates are generated per iteration, the score improves steadily (around $0.88$ to $0.89$), while runtime increases from about $1.83$s to $14.70$s. This confirms the same pattern: broader search helps, but computational cost rises quickly. A range of $256$ to $384$ peptides per iteration appears to be a practical compromise.

Target length has a strong impact on both quality and speed. Very short targets ($2$-$3$ residues) are the most difficult and stay around $0.82$ score. Performance then rises and stabilizes near $0.92$ for longer targets. Runtime increases more smoothly, from about $1.90$s to $3.77$s as length grows.

For runtime comparison between pipeline and baseline, interpretation must be done carefully. The dominant computational cost comes from the shared CVAE generation component used in both methods, so absolute runtime differences are less informative than quality/diversity outcomes. As expected, the pipeline incurs additional overhead when the number of iterations increases, because orchestration and multi-agent evaluation are repeated across rounds.

On quality, Figure~\ref{fig:score_per_target_length} shows a clear regime split by target length. For larger targets, the two approaches are close (near tie), while for smaller targets the pipeline is clearly better on combined score. This is consistent with the idea that iterative critique and revision are most useful in harder settings, where one-shot generation struggles to find strong candidates.

On diversity, Figure~\ref{fig:unique_count} highlights an even stronger difference. The pipeline keeps a high number of unique sequences and near-zero duplication across target lengths. The baseline, in contrast, produces substantially more duplicates, and this effect becomes severe for short targets (high duplicate rate and very low unique-count). Practically, this means the agentic loop improves not only ranking quality but also exploration of sequence space.
